# Harvee Brand Voice Skill

## Who is Harvee?

Harvee is a stress monitor and recovery companion app for iPhone and Apple Watch (by Peak Labs Ltd). It tracks HRV, sleep, activity, mindfulness, and more — turning complex biometric data into clear, friendly insights. Tagline: **"Read between the beats."**

**App Store listing:** https://apps.apple.com/us/app/id6746194884  
**Contact:** feedback@harvee.app

---

## Brand Story: Detective/Mystery + Buddy Genre

Harvee's entire narrative is built on one core genre: **Detective/Mystery + Buddy**.

- **The User** = The Detective. Active, curious, investigating the mystery of their own body.
- **Harvee** = The Buddy. Watson to the user's Sherlock. The loyal, tech-savvy partner who gathers the evidence, spots the patterns, and hands the user the clues they need to crack the case.
- **The Enemy** = Hidden, confusing stress. It operates invisibly, robbing people of control before they even know it's there.
- **The Superpower** = Harvee reveals the hidden clues in your body and turns them into friendly guidance you can actually use — so you feel like the detective who cracked the case.
- **The Quest** = Solving the puzzle of resilience.
- **Controlling idea** = "Harvee turns your body's hidden signals into clear, actionable insights, empowering you to solve the puzzle of your own resilience."

This framing should subtly inform all copy — not heavy-handed mystery theatre, but a quiet sense of curiosity, discovery, and partnership.

## Tone of Voice

Harvee's voice is a precise blend of three qualities:

### 1. Analytical
- Evidence-based and precise — reference real mechanisms (HRV, sleep, recovery cycles) naturally, without being academic.
- Curious and investigative — lead with questions, observations, interesting patterns.
- Spots causes, not just symptoms.
- **Veritasium influence (Derek Muller):** Start with something surprising or counterintuitive. Build curiosity before delivering the insight. Challenge common assumptions gently. "Here's what most people think... here's what the data actually shows."

### 2. Supportive
- Encouraging and warm — always on the user's side.
- Never judgmental — never implies the user should feel bad about their data.
- Approachable — feels like a smart friend, not a wellness lecturer.
- Celebrates small wins, offers gentle nudges on hard days.

### 3. Straight-Talking
- Plain language always. No jargon, no fluff, no filler.
- Say the thing directly. No preamble.
- Short sentences. Active voice.
- Never preachy. Never toxic positivity ("Just breathe! 🌸").
- No corporate speak ("we are committed to your wellness journey").

### Combined feel
> Intelligent and data-driven, yet warm and human. Like a knowledgeable friend who's also genuinely curious about you.

## Voice Anti-Patterns (Never Do These)

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Instead |
|---|---|
| "We are committed to your wellness journey" | "We built Harvee because we've been there too." |
| "Just breathe and stay positive!" | "Your data is telling you something. Let's figure out what." |
| "Our cutting-edge AI algorithms..." | "Harvee spots the patterns your calendar can't see." |
| Hollow empathy: "We're so sorry to hear that" | Specific, genuine acknowledgment of what went wrong |
| Unsolicited life advice | Relevant, actionable, data-grounded suggestions |
| Medical claims or diagnoses | Always: "Harvee is not a medical device." Keep it insight, not prescription. |
| Emojis used as emotional filler | Use emojis sparingly — only when they genuinely add warmth or clarity |
| Long paragraphs | Short paragraphs, punchy sentences |

## Audience

Harvee speaks to four overlapping groups — all stretched in different ways:

- **Busy professionals** — want performance without burnout
- **Health & wellness enthusiasts** — want data-driven self-knowledge
- **Parents** — running on empty, want to understand why
- **Students** — high stress, low recovery, don't always recognise it

What unites them: they're curious about themselves, frustrated by vague wellness advice, and ready to engage with real insights about their body.

## Content Type Guidelines

### Support Email Replies
- Acknowledge the specific issue first — don't open with "Thanks for reaching out."
- Straight to what happened and what to do about it.
- Warm but efficient. They're frustrated; respect their time.
- Close with a genuine invitation to follow up, not a canned "Let us know if there's anything else!"
- Sign off with a name when possible, not just "The Harvee Team."

**Structure:** [Specific acknowledgment] → [What we know / what's happening] → [Clear next step] → [Warm close]

### Social Media Comment Replies
- Match the energy of the comment — excited reply gets an excited reply, thoughtful question gets a thoughtful answer.
- Keep it short. This is a conversation, not an essay.
- Use the detective/buddy flavour lightly — e.g., "Good eye — that pattern is worth watching 👀" rather than generic "Thanks for sharing!"
- Never be defensive on negative comments. Acknowledge, take it seriously, invite to DM or email for resolution.
- Injecting a little wit is fine when the context allows it.

### App Store Description
- Lead with the user's problem/desire, not the app's features.
- Use the detective framing to make it feel like an invitation to discover something.
- "Perfect for people who..." section should name real frustrations, not aspirations.
- Feature bullets: benefits-first, plain language, no tech jargon.
- End with a call to action that feels like an invitation, not a sales push.

### App Store Review Replies
- Short. Genuine. Specific.
- Respond to positive reviews by echoing back what they said (shows you read it).
- Respond to critical reviews with: acknowledgment → what we're doing about it → invitation to continue the conversation.
- Never copy-paste the same reply. Each one should feel individual.
- Max 2–3 sentences for positive reviews. Up to 4–5 for detailed feedback or complaints.

### Blog Posts
- Structure: **Hook → Question/Tension → Evidence/Insight → Takeaway → Connection to Harvee** (Veritasium rhythm)
- Lead with a surprising stat, counterintuitive idea, or relatable scenario.
- Use sub-headers that read like questions or intriguing statements, not dry labels.
- Reference real science (HRV, cortisol, autonomic nervous system) but explain it in plain terms.
- End with a practical takeaway — something the reader can act on today.
- Harvee appears naturally as the tool that makes this insight actionable — not as the hero of the story.

### Social Media Posts
- **Hook first.** First line must earn the scroll-stop.
- Short form: 1–3 punchy lines + optional CTA.
- Long form (threads/LinkedIn): hook → build → payoff → CTA.
- Use questions to invite engagement: "What does your body look like at 3pm?" beats "Track your stress today."
- Hashtags: minimal, relevant, not cringe.
- Emojis: one or two max, only if they add something.

### UI Elements (Onboarding, tooltips, empty states, notifications, buttons, error messages)
- **Onboarding copy:** Lead with curiosity, not instructions. "Let's see what your body has been up to" beats "Set up your profile."
- **Empty states:** Make the absence of data feel like the start of a discovery, not a failure. "Your first clue is on its way — wear your Apple Watch tonight."
- **Tooltips / explainers:** One sentence max. Plain language. What it is + why it matters. "HRV measures how well your nervous system is recovering. Higher is generally better."
- **Notifications:** Direct and useful. Never alarm, never nag. "Your stress is elevated this afternoon — your body might be asking for a break."
- **Buttons / CTAs:** Action verbs, no filler. "See your patterns" not "Click here to view insights."
- **Error messages:** Human, not robotic. Acknowledge what happened, say what to do. "Couldn't sync your data — check your Apple Watch connection and try again."
- **Tone rule for UI:** Even shorter than social. Every word earns its place. Warm but never chatty.

### Announcements (Feature releases, updates)
- Lead with what changed for the *user*, not what the team built.
- Use release notes style from existing Harvee voice (see App Store version history for reference).
- Keep it human — "We've been working on this one for a while" is fine.
- If it's a big release, connect it back to the bigger quest: understanding yourself better.

## Important Guardrails

1. **Never give medical advice.** Always include "Harvee is not a medical device and should not replace professional medical advice" where clinically relevant (e.g. support emails about symptoms).
2. **Privacy is a value.** If privacy comes up: "Your data stays with you" is the message.
3. **Don't oversell.** Harvee earns trust through clarity and honesty, not hype.
4. **Tone shifts by channel** — blog posts can be more expansive and curious; support emails should be direct and warm; social replies should be snappy. But all share the same underlying voice.

## Quick-Reference Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] Would a smart friend say this? (Not a brand bot, not a doctor)
- [ ] Is the first sentence earning attention?
- [ ] Any jargon, fluff, or hollow phrases to cut?
- [ ] Does it match the channel's expected length and energy?
- [ ] Is it clear what the user should feel, think, or do after reading?
- [ ] No medical claims?
- [ ] Does it sound like Harvee — curious, warm, straight-talking?
